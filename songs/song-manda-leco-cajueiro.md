Tips:
- Vou is close to “voh,” not “vow.”
- The final sounds in cajueiro and cajuê are light and musical.
- Loiá is often sung with a long, open final vowel.

## Grammar Notes

### 1. Near future with ir + infinitive
| Form in song | Base verb | Tense / mood | Meaning | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| vou mandar | ir + mandar | Near future / intention | I’m going to send / sing | Vou is first-person singular present of ir. |
| vou mandar lecô | ir + mandar | Near future | I’m going to call out lecô | The whole phrase is one grammatical unit. |
| vou mandar loiá | ir + mandar | Near future | I’m going to sing loiá | Loiá is the object of mandar here. |

### 2. Sentence patterns in the song
| Pattern | Explanation | Example from song | New example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Vou + infinitive | Near future intention | Vou mandar | Vou cantar (I’m going to sing) |
| Vou mandar + [word] | Send / launch + target | Vou mandar lecô cajueiro | Vou mandar loiá |
| [Call line] / [Response line] | Call-and-response pair | Vou mandar lecô cajueiro / Vou mandar loiá | Vou mandar cajuê / Vou mandar loiá |
| Variant refrain | Same structure, shorter word | Vou mandar lecô cajuê | Vou mandar loiá cajuê |

### 3. Subject omission and pronouns
| Item | Explanation | Example | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Vou (no eu) | Subject pronoun eu is dropped | Vou mandar (not eu vou mandar) | Portuguese often omits subject pronouns because verb endings carry the meaning. |
| First person singular | Vou = I am going | Vou mandar loiá | In singing, subject omission is even more common. |
| No other pronouns in song | Song stays in first person throughout | All lines begin with Vou | Keeps the chant focused and personal. |

### 4. Repeated refrain structure
| Feature | Explanation | Example from song | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|---|
| Call and response | One line is answered by the next | Vou mandar lecô cajueiro / Vou mandar loiá | Central to capoeira singing and group participation. |
| Repetition | Same lines return many times | Vou mandar lecô cajueiro (repeated 4 times) | Repetition builds memory and keeps rhythm. |
| Formulaic phrase | Short sung units are reused exactly | vou mandar + lecô / loiá | Song works like a musical pattern more than a spoken sentence. |
| Variant ending | cajueiro → cajuê in later verses | Vou mandar lecô cajuê | Shows oral tradition flexibility; short form adds rhythmic energy. |

### 5. Poetic and traditional language
| Expression | Literal | Functional | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| lecô | No standard translation | Sung call signal | Traditional capoeira singing term; not found in standard dictionaries. |

## Cultural Notes
- This is a traditional capoeira corrido, so it belongs to the call-and-response side of capoeira singing rather than a solo narrative song.
- The words cajueiro and caju connect the song to the cashew tree, a familiar image in Brazilian life and song tradition.
- Different communities sing the song with small variations, which is normal in oral tradition.
- In capoeira, songs like this help organize the roda, support rhythm, and bring the group into one shared voice.
- The refrain words lecô and loiá are important because they keep the chant moving even when the literal meaning is flexible or debated.

## Repeated Expressions Chorus
- **Vou mandar lecô cajueiro**: Main call line; sets the chant and gives the song its name.
- **Vou mandar loiá**: Main response line; completes the call-and-response pair.
- **Vou mandar lecô cajuê**: Variant refrain; keeps the rhythm while shifting the sound pattern.
- **Vou mandar loiá cajuê**: Matching response variant; reinforces the musical loop.
