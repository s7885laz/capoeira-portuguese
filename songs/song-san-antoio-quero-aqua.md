| Eu quero água | EH-oo KEH-roo AH-gwah |

**Pronunciation tips for beginners:**
- *Santo* — the "s" sounds like English "s"; the final "o" sounds like "oo"
- *Antônio* — stress falls on the second syllable: ahn-TOH-nyoo; the "~" over "ô" marks nasalization (say it through your nose slightly)
- *água* — the "ã" is nasalized; sounds like "AH-gwah" with a nasal "ah"
- *pra* — a very short word, almost like a quick "pra" as in "pram" without the final "m"
- *benzer* — the "z" sounds like English "z"; stress on the last syllable: behn-ZEHR

## Grammar Overview

- **Main structures:** Simple subject-verb-object sentences (*Eu quero água*) and purpose clauses with *pra* + infinitive (*pra beber*, *pra lavar*, *pra benzer*)
- **Verb forms:** Present indicative (*quero*) and infinitives (*beber*, *lavar*, *benzer*)
- **Repeated patterns:** The entire song is built on one repeated frame: *Quero água pra [infinitive]* — varied only by the purpose verb
- **Useful connectors:** *pra* (for/to — purpose); *eu* (I)
- **Unusual or poetic expressions:** *benzer* carries a spiritual/religious meaning beyond just "bless" — it refers specifically to blessing water or a person with holy water using Catholic ritual

## Grammar Notes

### 1. Verb Forms

| Form in song | Base verb | Tense / Mood | Meaning | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| quero | querer | Present indicative | I want | 1st person singular; *querer* is an irregular verb |
| beber | beber | Infinitive | to drink | Used after *pra* to express purpose |
| lavar | lavar | Infinitive | to wash | Used after *pra* to express purpose |
| benzer | benzer | Infinitive | to bless | Used after *pra* to express purpose; slightly irregular in some conjugations |

### 2. Sentence Patterns

| Pattern | Explanation | Example from the song | New example |
|---|---|---|---|
| *Eu quero [noun]* | Subject + verb + direct object: "I want [something]" | *Eu quero água* | *Eu quero paz* (I want peace) |
| *Quero [noun] pra [infinitive]* | "I want [something] to [do something]" — *pra* + infinitive expresses purpose | *Quero água pra beber* | *Quero tempo pra descansar* (I want time to rest) |
| *Quero [noun] pra [infinitive 1] / pra [infinitive 2] / pra [infinitive 3]* | One core want expressed through three different purposes — creates lyrical escalation | *Quero água pra beber / pra lavar / pra benzer* | *Quero força pra lutar / pra vencer / pra viver* (I want strength to fight / to win / to live) |

### 3. Pronouns and Subject Omission

| Item | Explanation | Example from the song | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| *eu* (expressed) | In *Eu quero água*, the subject is explicitly stated for emphasis | *Eu quero água* | When *eu* is included, it adds personal emphasis: *I* (personally, specifically) want water |
| *eu* (omitted) | In *Quero água pra beber*, the subject pronoun is dropped — the verb ending *-o* already signals 1st person singular | *Quero água pra beber* | Both forms are correct; omitting the subject is more natural in rapid speech and song |

### 4. Repetition and Chorus Structure

| Feature | Explanation | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Call-and-response repetition | Each verse line is immediately repeated by the chorus — the simplest corrido structure | Solo: *Quero água pra beber* → Chorus repeats: *Quero água pra beber* |
| Anaphora (same opening) | Every verse begins with *Quero água pra…* — this repeated opening reinforces the single plea | *Quero água pra beber / pra lavar / pra benzer* |
| Three-part escalation | The three purposes (drink → wash → bless) move from physical to spiritual need | *beber* (body) → *lavar* (cleanliness) → *benzer* (soul) |
| Invocation opening | The song opens by directly addressing a saint: *Santo Antônio, eu quero…* | This follows the Afro-Brazilian and Catholic tradition of invoking divine figures for help |

### 5. Poetic / Traditional / Informal Language

| Expression | Literal meaning | Functional meaning | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| *pra* | for / to (purpose) | Contracted *para*; indicates purpose before an infinitive | Standard in all spoken and sung Brazilian Portuguese; never use in formal writing |
| *benzer* | to bless / to make holy | Specifically refers to blessing with holy water or making the sign of the cross over someone/something | Deeply rooted in both Catholic and Afro-Brazilian religious practice |
| *Santo Antônio* | Saint Anthony | The patron saint of Brazil, invoked for miracles, lost things, and protection | Addressed directly as if present — a form of prayer or invocation |
| *água benta* | holy water | (Not in lyrics but implied by *benzer*) | *Benzer* creates holy water (*água benta*) through the act of blessing — this is the spiritual water referenced |

### 6. Grammar Questions

- Why is *pra* used instead of *para* here? *Pra* is the contracted spoken form of *para* used universally in Brazilian Portuguese song and speech. Before an infinitive, *para* or *pra* signals purpose: "in order to."
- Why does the song alternate between *Santo Antônio eu quero água* (with *eu*) and *Quero água pra beber* (without *eu*)? The first line is an invocation — a direct address — so *eu* is emphasized: "I (personally) want water." The repeated verses drop *eu* for rhythmic flow, as the verb *-o* ending already communicates "I."
- What is the difference between *lavar* and *benzer*? *Lavar* is physical washing (cleaning the body or objects); *benzer* is spiritual — blessing with holy water. Together with *beber* (drinking), they cover the three dimensions of water: sustenance, purification, and sanctification.

## Cultural Notes

- **Santo Antônio (Saint Anthony of Padua)** is the patron saint of Brazil and one of the most beloved Catholic figures in Brazilian popular religion. He is invoked for miracles, finding lost objects and people, protection, and rain. Festivals in his honor (*Festa de Santo Antônio*) are celebrated across Brazil on June 13, especially in the Northeast.
- **Water in Afro-Brazilian spirituality** carries profound meaning. In Candomblé (the Afro-Brazilian religion that deeply shaped capoeira), water is associated with *Oxum* (goddess of fresh water, fertility, and love) and *Iemanjá* (goddess of the sea). Asking for water is never purely physical — it always carries spiritual weight.
- **Benzer (to bless)** refers to the act of blessing water to make it *água benta* (holy water), used in both Catholic rituals and Afro-Brazilian healing practices. A *benzedeira* (female folk healer) blesses water, herbs, and people to cure illness and ward off evil.
- **The three uses of water** — *beber* (drink), *lavar* (wash), *benzer* (bless) — represent a complete spiritual and physical cycle: sustaining the body, purifying it, and consecrating it. This progression is deeply meaningful in a culture where water equals life, cleanliness, and divine grace.
- **As a corrido**, this song is sung in the roda during the game. Its simplicity and repetition make it ideal for beginners and for maintaining the energy and rhythm of the roda. The directness of the lyrics — a simple plea to a saint — reflects the everyday blending of Catholic and African spiritual traditions in capoeira culture.
- The song is associated with the **Festa Junina** (June Festivals) season in Brazil, when Santo Antônio is celebrated. Capoeira groups often incorporate thematically relevant songs during this period.

## Repeated Expressions / Chorus

- **"Santo Antônio eu quero água"** — the opening invocation; addresses the saint directly and states the central plea
- **"Quero água pra beber / pra lavar / pra benzer"** — the three-part purpose verse; each repeated immediately by the chorus
- **"Eu quero água"** — the closing, stripped-down restatement; emotional and rhythmic conclusion of the cycle

## Practice

1. **Write 2 new sentences using song vocabulary:**
   - *Eu quero tempo pra descansar e pra aprender.* (I want time to rest and to learn.)
   - *Santo Antônio, eu quero paz pra viver.* (Saint Anthony, I want peace to live.)

2. **Reuse 1 sentence structure from the song in a new context:**
   - Song pattern: *Quero água pra beber / pra lavar / pra benzer.*
   - New sentence: *Quero música pra dançar, pra cantar, pra sentir.* (I want music to dance, to sing, to feel.)

3. **Identify 1 verb form and explain it:**
   - *Benzer* — infinitive form of the verb meaning "to bless." In the song it is used after *pra* to express purpose: *pra benzer* = "in order to bless." In full conjugation: *eu benzo* (I bless), *você benze* (you bless). It is slightly irregular in conjugation and carries a religious/spiritual meaning distinct from the more neutral *abençoar* (to bless generally).

4. **Recite or sing the chorus slowly:**
   - Practice the invocation: *SAHN-too ahn-TOH-nyoo EH-oo KEH-roo AH-gwah* — speak it slowly before singing.
   - Try the three-part sequence as a chant: *pra beh-BEHR / pra lah-VAHR / pra behn-ZEHR* — feel the rhythm of three equal beats.
   - In call-and-response with a partner: one person sings a verse, the other immediately repeats the exact same line.
