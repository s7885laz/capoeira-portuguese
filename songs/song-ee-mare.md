## RHYTHMIC SPECIALITY — STOP AND RESTART TECHNIQUE

This song is famous in the roda for its dramatic stopping and restarting.

### How it works
- The lead singer (mestre or contra-mestre) sings the call line
- The chorus responds — but at a certain moment, the song stops completely (full silence)
- The berimbau also stops, the game freezes
- Then, suddenly, the singer restarts with "Ie!" and the whole roda explodes back into motion
- This technique is called "parada" (stop/pause) in the context of the song

### Why it matters
- It tests the attention and reflexes of the players — they must be alert and resume immediately
- It creates dramatic tension and collective energy in the roda
- It is a teaching tool: a mestre uses it to observe who is truly present in the game
- The restart "Ie!" functions as a command and a release — the "Ie" shout is the traditional opening cry of Capoeira, inherited from African traditions

### For learners
When practicing this song, clap along and deliberately stop when the song stops — then re-enter on "Ie!" This builds your musical awareness (musicalidade) in the roda.

## Grammar Overview
- Main structures: Descriptive sentences with "estar" (ta) + adjective or gerund
- Verb forms: Past tense (subiu, desceu), present (chama, quer), gerunds (vazando, rolando)
- Repeated patterns: "Ie, mare" as call-and-response refrain; "Mare da beira mar" as anchor line
- Useful connectors: "no meio do" (in the middle of), "da" (from the / of the)
- Unusual/poetic expressions: "Ie!" as non-lexical vocalization with deep cultural function

## Grammar Notes

### 1. Verb Forms

| Form in song | Base verb | Tense / Mood | Meaning | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| subiu | subir | Preterite (past) | rose / went up | 3rd person singular |
| desceu | descer | Preterite (past) | fell / went down | 3rd person singular |
| chama | chamar | Present indicative | calls | 3rd person singular |
| quer | querer | Present indicative | wants | 3rd person singular; irregular verb |
| ta | estar | Present indicative (colloquial) | is | Contraction of "esta" |
| vazando | vazar | Present gerund | is ebbing | Used with "ta" — ta vazando |
| rolando | rolar | Present gerund | is rolling | Used with "ta" — ta rolando |

### 2. Sentence Patterns

| Pattern | Explanation | Example from song | New example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Subject + ta + adjective | Describes a current state | "A mare ta cheia" | "O mar ta calmo" (The sea is calm) |
| Subject + ta + gerund | Describes ongoing action | "A mare ta vazando" | "O barco ta chegando" (The boat is arriving) |
| Subject + verb + object pronoun | Action directed at someone | "Ela te chama" | "Ela te espera" (She waits for you) |
| Noun + ta + sozinho/a | Expresses isolation | "Meu barquinho ta sozinho" | "Eu to sozinho" (I am alone) |

### 3. Pronouns and Subject Omission

| Item | Explanation | Example from song | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| ela | She / It | "Ela te chama" | Refers to "a mare" — the tide is personified as feminine |
| te | you (object) | "Ela te quer" | Clitic object pronoun placed between subject and verb |
| meu | my (masc.) | "Meu barquinho" | Agrees in gender with the noun it modifies |
| Subject omission | No explicit subject for "Ie" | "Ie, mare!" | Pure vocalization — no grammatical subject |

### 4. Repetition and Chorus Structure

| Feature | Explanation | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Call-and-response | Lead sings a line, group responds | Lead: "A mare ta cheia" — Group: "Ie, mare" |
| Anchor refrain | Core line that anchors every section | "Mare da beira mar" |
| Stop-and-restart | Song pauses completely, resumes on "Ie!" | Full silence — "Ie! Ee mare..." |
| Word repetition | "Mare" repeated for rhythmic effect | "Oi de mare, mare" |

### 5. Poetic / Traditional / Informal Language

| Expression | Literal meaning | Functional meaning | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ie! | No literal meaning | Battle cry, call to attention, opening shout | African-rooted exclamation; marks start of roda or song section |
| ta | is | Colloquial present tense of "estar" | "Esta" is formal, "ta" is everyday spoken Brazilian Portuguese |
| barquinho | little boat | Term of smallness or endearment | Diminutive suffix "-inho" softens or endears the noun |
| beira mar | edge of the sea | Seashore | Poetic geographic reference; also a style of Capoeira Regional |
| ela te quer | she wants you | The tide calls / pulls you | Personification of the tide as a feminine, calling force |

### 6. Grammar Questions
- Why is "ta" used instead of "esta"? (Colloquial contraction — essential for understanding real Brazilian speech)
- Why does "cheia" end in "-a"? (Feminine adjective agreeing with "mare," a feminine noun)
- What does "te" mean in "ela te chama"? (Object pronoun "you" — the tide is calling YOU)
- What is "Ie" grammatically? (A non-lexical vocalization — culturally and musically functional, not a standard word)

## Cultural Notes
- "Ie Mare" / "Ee da Beira Mar" is one of the oldest and most widespread corridos in Capoeira, present in both Angola and Regional traditions
- The "Ie!" exclamation is rooted in African linguistic traditions brought to Brazil during the slave trade; it signals the start of a game, a challenge, or a section transition
- The sea and tide imagery (mare, beira mar, barco, mar) connects to Afro-Brazilian spiritual traditions — Iemanja, the orixa of the sea, is invoked through this water symbolism
- "Ela te chama, ela te quer" — the tide is personified as a feminine, calling force, possibly referencing both the sea and Iemanja herself
- The stop-and-restart in this song is used by mestres as a test of presence: students who keep moving or talking during the pause have failed the test of attention
- "Beira mar" also refers to a style of Capoeira Regional from the coastal cities of Bahia

---

## Repeated Expressions / Chorus
- "Ee mare, ee da beira mar" — the anchor line; the most recognizable phrase of the song
- "Ie, mare" — universal response line after each lead verse
- "Oi de mare, mare" — secondary refrain building rhythm and intensity
- "Ie!" — the restart signal after the full stop; the entire roda listens for this one word
