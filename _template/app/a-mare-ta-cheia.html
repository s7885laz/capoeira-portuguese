📚 Vocabulary

Every word from the song, explained:

Word Meaning Part of Speech Notes
maré tide noun (f) Feminine. Refers to ocean tide.
tá is (contracted form of "está") verb Present tense, 3rd person singular. "Está" = "is" (location/condition)
cheia full adjective Feminine form. Agrees with "maré" (feminine noun)
ê hey (rhythmic call) interjection Traditional Capoeira exclamation. Used to encourage rhythm.
olha look verb Imperative (command). "Olhar" = "to look"
só only, just adverb Can mean "only" or "just." Here: "look only at the fish"
o the article Masculine singular. "O peixe" = "the fish" (masculine)
peixe fish noun (m) Masculine. Singular. Common in Capoeira imagery (agility, movement)
que that, which relative pronoun Connects relative clause: "the fish that jumped"
pulou jumped verb Past tense (preterite), 3rd person singular. "Pular" = "to jump"
na in the (contraction of "em" + "a") preposition + article Feminine form. "Em" (in) + "a" (the, feminine)
areia sand noun (f) Feminine. The setting of this poetic image.

📖 Grammar

Verb Forms

Form in Song Base Verb Tense / Mood Meaning Notes
tá (está) estar Present indicative is (state/location) 3rd person singular. Contracted form common in speech.
olha olhar Imperative look! Command form, 2nd person (you/você). Positive command.
pulou pular Preterite (past) jumped 3rd person singular. Completed action in past.

Adjective Agreement

Adjective Noun Agreement Rule Example in Song
cheia (full) maré (tide - feminine) Adjectives agree with noun gender. Feminine noun = feminine adjective. "A maré cheia" (not "cheio" which is masculine)

Sentence Structure

Pattern Explanation Example from Song New Example
Subject + Verb + Adjective Simple descriptive sentences. The subject (A maré) + verb (tá) + quality (cheia). "A maré tá cheia" = The tide is full "A agua tá fria" = The water is cold
Verb + Article + Noun Imperative command with object. Verb first, then what to look at. "Olha só o peixe" = Look only at the fish "Olha a lua" = Look at the moon
Relative Clause: Noun + que + Verb Describes a noun with "that" or "which." The "que" connects the noun to what it does. "o peixe que pulou" = the fish that jumped "o menino que dança" = the boy that dances

Poetic & Traditional Language

Expression Literal Meaning How It's Used Cultural Context
"A maré tá cheia" The tide is full Describes the ocean at high tide. Used metaphorically in Capoeira to represent abundance, fullness, energy. Ocean imagery is common in Capoeira songs, connecting to African maritime traditions and the history of enslaved people near the coast.
"O peixe que pulou" The fish that jumped Fish jumping is symbolic of agility, freedom, and quick movement—qualities valued in Capoeira. Animal imagery is central to Capoeira—the ginga (basic movement) mimics natural creatures.
"Ê, ê, ê" Hey! Hey! Hey! A rhythmic call used in Capoeira to encourage movement and maintain rhythm during the roda (circle). These exclamations come from African call-and-response traditions, central to Capoeira culture.

🌍 Cultural Context

Origin: "A Maré Tá Cheia" is a traditional Capoeira corrido (song with a leader and chorus). Its exact origins are unknown, but it's part of the Capoeira Angola tradition, emphasizing connection to water, nature, and freedom.

Significance: The song reflects the African diaspora's deep connection to the ocean—many Africans arrived in Brazil by sea, and water symbolizes freedom, cleansing, and spiritual power in African traditions.

Tradition: This corrido is widely taught in Capoeira schools because of its simple vocabulary, strong rhythm, and rich metaphorical content. It's appropriate for beginners but carries profound cultural meaning.

Usage: Traditionally sung during the roda (circle), with the mestre (master) singing the lead and students joining in the chorus. The rhythm encourages flowing movements and celebrates the natural world.

🔄 Chorus & Repeated Expressions

  • "A maré tá cheia" — The main call. Establishes the central image and rhythm. Repeated multiple times for emphasis and to maintain the roda's beat.
  • "Ê, ê, ê" — Response or rhythmic filler. Keeps the energy up and allows singers to breathe between phrases.
  • "Olha só o peixe / Que pulou na areia" — The narrative verses. Leaders may improvise variations here while keeping this core image.
